  ---
  The Short Answer: GPOs Create as Much Fragmentation as They Prevent

  Here are the five structural reasons vendor sprawl persists:

  1. Maverick Spend Is Massive (and Industry-Wide)

  Maverick spend = buying outside negotiated contracts. The numbers:
  - ~20% of indirect procurement costs flows through off-contract buying
  - Costs organizations 5–16% of negotiated savings annually
  - Only top-performing orgs achieve 90%+ compliance — meaning most hotels don't

  This is the specific, named industry problem. 1 in 5 dollars is going to
  vendors nobody negotiated with.

  2. GPOs Only Cover the "Easy" Categories

  GPO contracts are built around:
  - Food & beverage (the big one)
  - Branded consumables
  - Standard, negotiable categories

  GPOs don't cover:
  - Local maintenance vendors
  - FF&E (furniture, fixtures, equipment)
  - Specialty / niche items needed for brand differentiation
  - Most services (landscaping, HVAC, janitorial, IT, local contractors)
  - One-off purchases

  So even a fully-compliant GPO member has 40–60% of their spend running through
   a chaotic long tail that nobody is managing.

  3. GPO Rules Actually Create Off-Contract Premiums

  This one is counterintuitive but important. From the research:

  ▎ "Avendra's supplier agreements are negotiated based on aggregate demand from
  ▎  its biggest members. If a property needs a niche product that falls outside
  ▎  those contracts, you either go off-contract or settle for a substitute."

  ▎ "When a property requires products outside that network, the 'free' 
  ▎ membership becomes expensive due to off-contract premiums."

  GPO vendors are prohibited from offering below-contract pricing — even when
  they'd like to. So GMs who find better deals elsewhere get penalized by the
  GPO structure itself.

  4. Hotels Have a Five-Stakeholder Decision Problem

  Purchasing decisions in hotels aren't made by one person. There are:
  1. Owners (asset owners like Kamba / Dada)
  2. Management companies (Dora Hotel Company, White Lodging)
  3. Brand (Hilton, Marriott mandates)
  4. GPO (Avendra, Foodbuy)
  5. Individual property GM (has local autonomy)

  Each stakeholder has different purchasing authority and preferences. The
  result: the same category gets bought through 3 different channels depending
  on who decided. That's structural fragmentation baked into the industry.

  5. GPOs Favor Scale — Mid-Market Gets the Worst Terms

  ▎ "Scale-driven contracts favor large chains. For independent and boutique 
  ▎ operators, Avendra's structural advantages become structural friction."

  Mid-market hotel groups (10–50 properties) are in the worst GPO position — big
   enough to join, too small to negotiate favorable tiers. They get the baseline
   rates while the big chains get the deep cuts.
